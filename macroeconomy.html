📌 Present Perfect: Simple vs. Progressive

Present Perfect Simple (has/have + past participle):

  • Emphasizes completed actions and their current results.
  • Focuses on quantities (how much / how many).
  • Ex: The central bank has cut the interest rate twice this year.

Present Perfect Progressive (has/have + been + verb-ing):

  • Emphasizes ongoing processes, duration, and unfinished actions.
  • Focuses on how long an activity has been happening.
  • Ex: Inflation has been rising steadily for the last six months.

Read the opinion column.
Green Power: The Shift in Colombia's Energy Matrix
Opinion Column · Economic Times · By L. Suarez

Over the last four years, the Colombian government has been aggressively pushing for a transition toward renewable energies. This paradigm shift has not been easy. For decades, the national trade balance has heavily relied on fossil fuel exports, primarily oil and coal. Consequently, the recent policy changes have sparked intense debate between the public and private sectors regarding the country's macroeconomic stability.

The numbers, however, tell a complex story. On one hand, the Ministry of Energy has approved over 40 new wind and solar projects in the northern coastal regions. Private sector alliances have invested billions, and productivity in clean tech has reached an all-time high. The government argues that this transition has been creating sustainable social welfare and lowering long-term debt. They claim that reducing our dependency on oil has shielded our GDP per capita from the volatile global markets.

Conversely, critics point out that the immediate drop in oil revenue has heavily impacted the fiscal deficit. Because the state has been subsidizing these new green initiatives while simultaneously losing traditional export revenue, the national debt has been creeping up. Furthermore, consumer confidence has dropped slightly as energy prices have fluctuated during this transitional period. Ultimately, while the nation has secured a greener future, the short-term economic shockwaves are still being felt.
